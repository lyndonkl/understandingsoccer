The Two Meanings
of 26¢

At 9:00 PM, a contract paying $1 if Switzerland knocked Argentina out of the World Cup cost 26¢. Two hours later, after a goal, an equalizer, and a red card, it cost 26¢ again.

It was not the same price.

scroll ↓

First: the naive reading

A price is a count

A Kalshi contract settles at $1 if the event happens, $0 if it doesn’t. So a price of 26¢ real reads like a probability: of every 100 ways tonight could go, the crowd trades as if about 26 end with Switzerland advancing.

Each dot here is one ten-thousandth of that belief: one possible ending of the match. 2,600 are lit. This is the market’s claim, drawn as a census of futures.

Why the naive reading is incomplete

Nobody set this number

There is no oracle behind the curtain. Kalshi is an exchange. Buyers who will pay at most 54¢ rest on one side of the book; sellers who will accept no less than 55¢ rest on the other. The one-cent gap between them is called the spread. While everyone waits, nothing trades.

The queue is not all amateurs, either. Susquehanna has quoted Kalshi markets since 2024, and Kalshi’s own trading affiliate participates.

Beside this card: the actual resting orders in this week’s semifinal market, England v Argentina real, captured July 13, 2026 from the public API.

How a price becomes a print

The tape answers the question

A resting order changes nothing. The price moves only when someone stops waiting: a buyer takes the sellers’ 55¢, or a seller hits the buyers’ 54¢. Each acceptance prints a trade. The last print is the price you read.

The public tape shows which side blinked. The thirty most recent prints in this market are all buyers crossing at 55¢ real. That is why the price sits at 55: eager buyers keep accepting the most reluctant sellers’ terms.

7.9 million contracts rest at 55¢ real. If buyers eat through them all, the next print reads 56¢. A price moves one consumed level at a time.

The model’s two ingredients

Two dice, loaded differently

Before any grid, meet its ingredients. The model treats each team’s goals as random arrivals at a steady average rate: λ, in goals per 90 minutes. Fix the rate and the arithmetic of random arrivals fixes the chance of every count, the way a shop averaging 1.8 customers an hour still has a knowable chance of an empty hour. That recipe is the Poisson distribution.

So Argentina at λ ≈ 1.8 per match model works out to: 16% no goals, 29% one, 27% two, 16% three, 11% more. Switzerland at λ ≈ 0.9 model: 42% blank, 36% one goal, 16% two, 6% more.

Those are the two bars beside this card, drawn in dots. Argentina’s mass sits on one and two goals; Switzerland’s crowds zero and one. Multiply the two dice and every final score follows.

The first 26¢ · 9:00 pm

At kickoff, 26¢ means capability

Ninety-plus minutes ahead, eleven against eleven, score 0–0. The market had opened the week at 27–28¢ and sat at 25–26¢ all match day real.

Multiply the dice and the grid appears: every final score, sized by its share of the 10,000 endings model. Each cell’s dot count is Argentina’s row share times Switzerland’s column share, times the 10,000 dots. A 1–0 Argentina win is the single likeliest score, near 12%. Switzerland’s lit mass collects where its column beats Argentina’s row, plus a slice of every draw cell, because a level score at ninety sends a knockout tie to extra time.

Count the lit dots and you get the price. This 26¢ is cold: capability over a long, high-variance horizon.

The match begins to happen

Minute 10: the favorite scores

Alexis Mac Allister heads in a Messi corner. The contract collapses from 25¢ to 12¢ within minutes real.

Switzerland’s ability did not change; the ledger did. The rates simply shrink to the time left: 1.8 × 80⁄90 ≈ 1.6 more Argentina goals expected, 0.9 × 80⁄90 ≈ 0.8 Swiss ones model. At that Swiss rate, the chance of scoring at least once in the remaining eighty minutes is 54% model; and even level again, Switzerland would face the same stronger opponent with less clock. Re-run the whole grid from 1–0 with eighty minutes left and Argentina wins in regulation 83% of the time model.

The cold read of this state: about 10¢ model. The market paid 12¢ real.

The most crowded minute

Minute 67: 1–1, and the market floods

Dan Ndoye slips the equalizer under Martínez. Remember the tape: a minute holds many individual prints. In this one minute, prints in the Switzerland-to-advance contract (the YES side we have followed all night) land anywhere from 10¢ to 35¢ real: different pairs of traders, same goal, twenty-five cents of disagreement.

The next minute, 1.28 million contracts change hands, roughly 22× the pre-kickoff rate real. Heavy volume alone proves interest. Heavy volume across a 25-cent range proves disagreement: a flood of orders repricing the same goal faster than the crowd can agree on what it is worth (the public tape shows over a thousand prints in three minutes real).

The pale band around the price line is each minute’s full traded range. Watch it blow open here, and stay open.

The alarm

Minute 72: red card

Breel Embolo, already booked, goes down too easily. VAR review. Second yellow. Switzerland will finish the night with ten men.

The equalizer’s hope is repriced within two minutes: 32¢ → 16¢ real. A team that needed goals now cannot spare the bodies to chase one.

But watch what the price does next. The same crippled state has a second, colder reading.

Two readings of ten men

The story and the clock

The story reading: Switzerland is down a man for fifty-plus minutes, hope just cancelled, sell.

The clock reading: Argentina now has fewer minutes in which to convert its advantage, and every scoreless minute makes the draw, and the penalty shootout behind it, more likely.

The dashed line is the clock reading made precise. It is the same two dice from kickoff, re-rolled every minute: less time on the clock, and after the red card Switzerland’s scoring rate roughly halved while Argentina’s more than doubles. Rolled from this state, the dice value survival at ≈12¢, drifting toward 17¢ at the whistle model. The solid white line is the market: the traded Kalshi price.

Who holds each reading? Kalshi does not disclose who trades. Treat the two readings as an interpretation of the price’s behavior, and never as a census of traders.

Time decay, live

Minutes 73–90: the survival climb

Switzerland stops trying to win. Ten men, one objective: hold 1–1 to the whistle. The price climbs a cent at a time, 16 → 19 → 22 → 25 real.

Now read the two lines together. Re-rolling the dice each minute, time alone lifts fair value from ≈12¢ to 17¢ model. The traded price climbed from 16¢ to 26¢ real. The amber wedge between the lines is the part the clock cannot explain. It gets a name at the final whistle.

Same digits, third meaning, one screen over: at minute ~85 Argentina’s regulation-time win contract also read 26¢ real. A favorite racing the same clock from the other side.

The second 26¢ · ~11:05 pm

At the whistle, 26¢ means survival

Regulation ends 1–1. The contract costs 26¢ again real, and every world inside it has been replaced.

The capability worlds are gone. What remains: score against a scoring-rate tilt the model puts near nine to one (rare), or hold 0–0 for thirty more minutes and take Argentina to penalties, roughly a coin flip model.

Kickoff’s 26¢ was a bet on ability across time. This 26¢ is a bet against time itself. Same number, rebuilt from different stuff.

Reading the gap

The nine cents of feeling

A cold model prices this endgame near 17¢ model: about 4¢ of “ten men win extra time” plus 13¢ of “reach penalties, flip the coin.” The crowd paid 26¢ real.

The 9¢ band on top is the wedge you watched grow, and the model cannot see inside it. Some of it may be feeling: the narrative premium of heroic defiance on TV; holders who bought at 28¢ refusing to sell at a loss. Some of it may be knowledge: legs, bodies, a low block that looked unbreakable. A price never tells you which.

Honesty notes: the gap runs both ways (at the equalizer the market lagged below this model, 32¢ real vs 35% model); fees explain at most 1.75¢ real; and the model itself is an assumption, calibrated to the kickoff price with red-card effects from published World Cup research.

Extra time

The ending that arrived

The ten men hold, and hold; the price touches 35¢ as penalties near real. Then, minute 112: Julián Álvarez curls one into the top corner. 35¢ → 2¢ in one minute. Lautaro adds a third. The contract settles at $0.

Was the 26¢ wrong? No. A 26% event fails to arrive 74% of the time; judging a price by one outcome is the oldest mistake in probability. What the market actually sold, all night, was meaning: a live translation of the game state into one number.

The lens, elsewhere · Washington, fall 2025

Two 17¢, forty-three days apart

The 2025 government shutdown became the longest in history: 43 days. Kalshi ran markets on its length.

Sept 23, eight days before it began: “lasts >35 days” traded at 17¢ real. A lottery ticket on the unthinkable, when the median forecast was 4½ days. It settled YES.

Nov 5, day 36, record just broken, fourteen failed votes deep: “lasts >60 days” traded at 17¢ real. An exhaustion tail on a crisis with no visible exit. It settled NO.

Same number. One priced “surely not,” the other “why would it ever end.” You cannot read either without the date attached.

The lens, elsewhere · New York, fall 2025

A survival price in slow motion

Kalshi’s market on the NYC mayoral race spent October drifting 85¢ → 91¢ → 92¢ → 94¢ real for Mamdani, with no comparable news. The climb was the clock: calendar days draining from any comeback, the same time decay that lifted ten-man Switzerland, stretched over weeks.

And when Eric Adams, the event that was supposed to threaten Mamdani, actually withdrew on Sept 28, the price went up: 80¢ → 84¢ real. The missing 20¢ had been structural uncertainty about the field, and the event revealed what the number had been made of.

Julian Jaynes, and the point

Understanding is metaphor

Jaynes argued that we understand anything by mapping it onto something familiar enough to feel. A market price is the unfamiliar thing: one number compressing capability, survival, structure, panic, conviction, and time.

The familiar thing is the event itself: the pitch, the clock, the man down, the calendar. Translate the number back into its state of the world and it opens; read it bare and it lies to you politely.

26¢ never meant “26%.” It meant these worlds, counted, right now. One price is never one thing.

The model (labeled wherever it appears)

  • Structure: independent double-Poisson match model (Maher 1982; Dixon & Coles 1997 lineage), time-scaled in play; extra time at ⅓ of full-match rates; penalties treated as 50/50 (assumption).
  • Calibration: total goals 2.7 per match (WC 2018 ≈ 2.64, WC 2022 ≈ 2.69, Frontiers in Sports & Active Living 2024; the 2026 tournament is running hotter, ≈ 2.9); λ split solved so that P(Switzerland advances) equals the real kickoff price of 26¢ → Argentina 1.84, Switzerland 0.87.
  • Red card: sanctioned team’s scoring rate × 0.53, opponent × 2.24 (Červený, van Ours & van Tuijl 2017, Empirical Economics, 320 World Cup matches). Sanity anchor: of EPL teams drawing when reduced to ten, 65% lost / 30% drew / 5% won (Chowdhury 2015).
  • Fair-value overlay: the model re-evaluated each minute along the real timeline using only the verified state (score, red card, clock). The match-clock-to-UTC mapping is inferred, anchored at the verified event candles (kickoff 01:00, goal 01:11, equalizer 02:29, red card 02:33, whistle 03:05, ET goals 03:33/03:44), with halftime and the pre-ET break held flat. Conditional values quoted on cards: P(advance | 0–1 at 10′) ≈ 10¢; P(Argentina wins in regulation | 1–0 at 10′) ≈ 83%; fair value 12¢ at the red card → 17¢ at the whistle.
  • Under these assumptions the model’s late-game fair value is ≈ 17¢ vs the traded 26¢. The 9¢ gap is presented as an interpretation, and alternative published multipliers (Titman et al. 2015; Ridder et al. 1994; Maia et al. 2023) would move it by a few cents either way.
  • Who trades: Kalshi does not disclose trader composition (Sportico 2025). The “story reading vs clock reading” framing is an interpretation of price behavior, and the page says so where it appears.

Exchange mechanics

  • Binary $1/$0 contracts, 1–99¢ pricing, YES/NO complementarity, central limit order book: Kalshi’s own explainer and Help Center; confirmed live via the public API (market_type: binary, notional_value: $1).
  • Fees ≈ 0.07 × P × (1−P) per contract, at most 1.75¢ at 50¢ (Kalshi Help Center; pm.wiki; API fee_type: quadratic).
  • Participants include institutional market makers, firms that keep standing buy and sell quotes resting in the book (Susquehanna, announced by Kalshi April 2024), and Kalshi’s own trading affiliate (Sportico, 2025). CFTC-designated contract market since Nov 2020; sports contracts since Jan 2025; Third Circuit ruling April 2026; litigation ongoing in several states.
